The Unexpected Return of Candy Corn

By Joli Holmes

November 1st, 2021 10:05 a.m. ET
3:43 a.m.

Candystore.com releases tabulated totals of pounds of candy from the 2021 Halloween cycle as well as their rankings by state.

4:00 a.m.

Data reaches the hands of skilled experts at Candy Metrics and Analysis Company (CMAC). Experts reported that they merged data from Candystore.com with the most recent US decennial census, 2020, results to derive the most recent estimates.

7:45 a.m.

Cindy Miyamota, CEO of CMAC announces the company will be delivering official results within the hour.

8:43 a.m.

Press conference called by Miyamota begins and the revelation that Candy Corn remains a favorite is announced. Miyamota says,

"Thankfully, we can report that in zero states Candy Corn ranked first. However, a shocking 14 states Candy Corn ranked as a second or third favorite. We're particularly concerned about some states in the West, where Candy Corn favoritism* was highest as a percentage of states. We alerted authorities in these states to be prepared. We've also warned authorities to be ready in Michigan and New Mexico. Although these states do not a high purchase rate per capita, they ranked Candy Corn in second place overall, across all other candies.**"
* 38.5% of western states ranked candy corn as second or third, compared to 33% in the Midwest, 33% in the Northeast and 12.5% in the South.
** Alabama, Idaho, Iowa, Maine, Nevada, New York, North Dakota, Oregon, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota and Utah ranked Candy Corn third.

On November 1st, 2021, Americans awoke to disturbing news. Unbeknownst to many, Candy Corn is still popular candy. This revelation has sent shock waves through towns and cities across the United States; people had once believed the days of Candy Corn were behind them.

The statistics released by the Candy Metric and Analysis Company (CMAC) were particularly alarming in Nevada, North Dakota, and Utah. CMAC is responsible for collecting data on candy consumption and currently monitors purchasing of twenty-four different candy types.

CMAC's estimates showed that Nevada residents bought Candy Corn at a rate of 1 pound per every 20 residents. In North Dakota and Utah, the statistics are even worse. North Dakotans bought Candy Corn at a rate of 1 pound per every 15 residents and Utonians bought Candy Corn at a rate of 1 pound per every 13 residents. These states are classified as high consumption per capita and are under close watch by Federal and State authorities.

Dr. Linda Blessing, the director of the Center for Candy Control (CCC), reviewed the statistical data released this morning. She conveyed additional concern for the West, "Not only was the state favoritism much higher in the West in general, but rate of Candy Corn purchased per capita in West makes us more concerned." Analysis showed that people from western states purchased 1 pound of Candy Corn per every 24 residents. All other regions purchased Candy Corn at less than half that rate. The next highest region is the South which purchased candy corn at a rate 1 pound per every 59.4 residents. These statistics translate into 4.2 pounds purchased per every 100 people in the West compared to only 1.7 pounds purchased per every 100 people in the South.
